Soft Skills & Online Learning in Design

"User Centered e-Learning: Methods to Enhance and Assess Soft Skills for Online Learning in Design" was a research project for eCampusOntario, publicly funded by the Province of Ontario, that explored the role of online learning, positive or negative, in supporting essential Employability Skills (EES) and Vocational Learning Outcomes (VLO) for education in design.

My Role

User Experience Researcher

Goals

The creation of an evaluation framework and assessment criteria that can guide the effective use of online materials (i.e. Lynda.com) as well as measurement of these online materials’ effectiveness in their support of soft skills considered essential by design employers to the success of design students.

Type & Duration

Team Project - 12 months

Process

Research Objectives

  • Explore design students’ interaction with online videos: patterns of use, expectations and challenges
  • Develop an understanding of soft skills required by design industry employers
  • Identify key EESs and VLOs of importance to selected design-focused courses
  • Develop an evaluation framework that can inform the design of hybrid/online learning material to facilitate the assessment of online videos for enhancing selected soft skills and supporting EESs and VLOs

Method & Participants

Employers

In this phase, we interviewed 20 design leaders in the design industry, from small, medium and large companies. Some of their roles include Founders, CEOs, Directors, and Team Leads.

During the interviews with employers, we aimed to explore:
  • The employer’s opinion on the most important soft skills for a designer
  • How prepared employers think junior designers are in regards to their soft skills
  • A comparison between the soft skills of employees who have been hired straight out of post-secondary schools versus those who have more than 3 years of work experience
  • Any soft skills that employers felt designers (both junior and senior) could improve on
Sample questions:
  • Do you feel that junior designers hired directly from college or university lack any soft skills compared to those with a job experience?
  • In an interview process, what soft skills do you think are essential in a candidate? (meaning, you wouldn't hire them if they don't possess such skills).
  • What soft skills do you feel junior designers need to improve on?
  • What soft skills do you feel senior designers need to improve on?

Students

In this phase, the team conducted 4 focus groups with a total of 20 students. Students were enrolled in different semesters of undergraduate and graduate design programs.

During the student focus groups, we aimed to explore:
  • Students’ patterns and behaviours when using online videos
  • Their experience and impression of current e-learning resources
  • How developed they think their own soft skills are
  • How they would like to be taught soft skills online
Sample discussion questions:
  • What soft skills do you think you should have to succeed in the industry?
  • What engages you while learning in-class vs online?
  • How can online learning content be improved to be more engaging?
  • Do you think it’s possible to incorporate teaching and practice soft skills into e-learning platforms?

Faculty

In this phase, 10 educators in design programs were interviewed.

With the faculty interviews we aimed to explore:
  • Their impression of current e-learning resources, and how they utilize them in conjunction with their in-class coursework
  • Their opinion on the most important soft skills for designers
  • Their opinions on the strength of the soft skills of the students they have taught
  • How (and if) they think soft skills can be effectively taught online
Questions sample:
  • Do you use online videos to supplement your teaching? In what ways? (i.e.: in-class, or assigned homework).
  • Which soft skills do you think are important for a designer to succeed in the industry?
  • Are there any changes you would like to see in the curriculum to help students develop soft skills?

Synthesis & Analysis

After collecting all the data, the interviews were transcribed, coded, and analyzed on NVivo. Coding was done by multiple analysts to ensure inter-reliability. The interviews were coded through nodes that corresponded to the EES (Essential Employability Skills).

After all the interviews had been conducted and their coding completed, we started analyzing all the information. In addition to the qualitative data, this phase provided two sets of quantitative data: firstly, a chart that quantified how frequently each soft skill was mentioned as essential to a designer; secondly, a chart that showcased the results of the Likert scale survey.

Findings

Employers

What are soft skills for employers?
Soft skills are anything that is not technical, how people conduct themselves and the work they do, and even things like professionalism are very dependent on soft skills. How a person can conduct themselves professionally. Soft skills are skills that I think can't be taught. But, they're skills that allow you to interact with people. It's a mentality of thinking that everyone is, as a collective, going to succeed. Thinking about what you do as a team and not like as an individual.
What are the essential soft skills a designer needs to succeed in the industry?

From the coding of the qualitative data, we created a rank of the most important soft skills.

  1. Communication
  2. Team Work
  3. Critical Thinking and Problem Solving
  4. Speaking
  5. Attitude
Qualitative Analysis

With hours of interviews on hand, we found ourselves with a well of valuable information on several different topics.
I'm gonna present some of the points touched on by the participants, that greatly influenced the final deliverables of the project.

Communication

Communication is the skill that design employers consider the most important for designers - from junior to senior roles. Here are some of their thoughts:

We go back to communication as one of the key drivers of being a designer. I think if you can convince somebody that what you're proposing is a good idea and that it has value, and that it has tangible desirability, and that it's feasible, then you create a much more compelling case for why that should be implemented. Tell me the theory behind what it is that you’ve done and how you got to that theory. I think there is, from my experience, a real emphasis on ‘look at my final product’, whereas I wonder ‘ok, well how did you get there?’ That just comes down to good communication and what we’re looking for. As a designer if you can't communicate your ideas, that's it, you're dead, right?
Team Work

Team work is the second most mentioned soft skill that employers consider important for designers to have. According to design leaders, collaboration is so important because designers often act as a bridge between different disciplines involved in a product's lifecycle:

I think most of the time, students are just concerned about the work and then when they get put into a work environment, they’re not really aware that there’s this whole other side of things, where things like collaboration are important and just being able to work with different people who have different attitudes or different backgrounds. (...) you see designers in finance, you see designers in health, you see designers in all these other disciplines. And the designer accesses this bridge between an understanding of these disciplines and trying to make connections between them that people otherwise might not make. So, I think those are very tangible soft skills that are extremely important in building for the future of designers. (...) [a designer] is not going to solve a problem or conquer the world's problems on your own. It's a mentality of being, or thinking, that everyone is as collective going to succeed. Thinking about what you do as a team and not like as an individual.

Students

What are soft skills for students?
Skills that can't really be taught. You just learn by experience. Not Adobe skills. How to behave when you're in a meeting, how to behave when you have some struggle with a client, especially in design teams where you need to constantly tell your client which way to follow and why you think that way.
Thoughts on Classroom Education vs Online Education

Some students talked about how traditional classroom settings can be distracting and obtrusive to learning. Utilizing e-learning videos to supplement technical classes can help students better understand the content and the skills required to excel in the course and/or program.

I think it’s easy to get distracted in class, especially if people are talking or you can’t really hear the prof. So, with a video, you can just go back and you can fully hear. I think it also saves time for the professor, instead of them having to go step-by-step. We can just review the videos at home and then ask any questions about it during class. For some classrooms, you can’t really hear or see properly, but for videos you have the demonstration happening right in front of you.
Online Learning and Soft Skills

During the focus groups, we found out that the unbiased opinion of students is that soft skills cannot be learned online.

[When I develop my soft skills, it’s mostly in-class. The benefit is because I see you and you see me – I don’t get that when I’m learning something through Lynda. I would lean more towards in-class because I would like to get feedback and comments from the professor, and that’s not something you can do with the online videos. I feel like you lose a lot of the classroom dynamics during e-learning. I mean, how would you learn team work or presentation online?

Faculty

What are soft skills for faculty?
So talking from my perspective, soft skills are the skills that students and people need to be successful in their life. They are different from our technical skills. I think that soft skills are things that are lifelong learning. No student can expect from George Brown [College] or any university or other college or institution that they will be perfect at soft skills.
Views on Online Learning

Most of the faculty members think that students would better thrive in a hybrid setting rather than full online learning. Although this may be true, there's a contrasting idea from another professor about the relationship between online learning and accountability:

I don't know, maybe this is just my perception but I do feel like e-learning is good to a certain degree. I feel like if it's always e-learning or videos, you just lose that applied skill. I feel that there needs to be a balance otherwise you can get lost in a video and never apply your skills. I feel like you need that sort of instructor to guide you in a direction, even as you apply those skills. You require self-discipline to be able to engage and the barrier is that I think students are not learning self-discipline the hardest thing to learn early skills by taking an online tutorial for part of your course that helps you pass your GRE or whatever. It's going to really really help you is it how hard is to get yourself to sit in front of the screen when it's not something. When the [course] delivery is online, the accountability is on the students. When it's an in-class thing, they come to the class, they play with their phones, I can't engage for the whole three hours. But if you're doing it in your own home, and you take the time to focus because the accountability is on you, you don't text and watch my videos, you are most likely just watching the video.

Triangulation

Evaluation Framework for Online Courses

Based on the qualitative research, a framework was developed to help educators and content developers gauge the effectiveness of online courses in:

  • Engaging students towards completion of the course, and
  • Promoting learning and practice of soft skills.


The following five items were the top recommendations from students, educators, and employers on how to effectively utilize online videos for design-related disciplines.

  1. Optimal length of video: between 5 and 20 minutes
  2. Gamified learning
  3. Embedded interaction with instructors
  4. Embedded collaboration between students towards completing assignments
  5. Exercises with soft skills related activities

Whole courses and/or individual videos could be assessed using the framework presented above, in order to find out how effective they would be in engaging students and promoting the learning & practice of essential soft skills.

Trial Application

Having created the assessment framework, the team evaluated 4 online videos from different platforms. For each criterion, the videos were given a score from 1 to 5.

Videos that had a minimum total score of 60% (15 points, out of 25 points) were considered acceptable. The following rubric was employed:

  • 1 point: Does not meet the criteria
  • 2 points: Inconsistently meets the criteria and/or soft skills activities are not intrinsic to completing the video
  • 3 points: Meets the criteria, but could be more engaging
  • 4 points: Meets the criteria and creates an engaging experience
  • 5 points: Meets the criteria and excels at creating superiorly engaging and impactful soft skill activities

Limitations

Although we were able to gather enough data to provide us with a strong foundation for our triangulation and framework, there were limitations that prevented us from expanding our data collection. They were:

  • Difficulties with recruiting participants – we had limited data from employers in large companies
  • The impact from the strike in Ontario's colleges in 2017 restricted our access to recruiting more students
  • Narrow access to a limited range of design programs, which prevented us from gathering insights from participants of a broader design background

Next steps

The work en route to a future where students are provided with online platforms that are more engaging towards the learning and practice of soft skills does not end with this project.

Our findings, ultimately, provided us with a design question that deserves a research project itself:


How might we design an e-learning platform for design students in order to better engage them in learning and practicing communication, team work, critical thinking and problem solving – soft skills that are essential for their future careers?
